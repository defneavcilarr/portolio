Medical Device · 2022

Automated Sharps Box

An automated sharps disposal prototype engineered to eliminate the risk of needlestick injuries in clinical settings designed during an internship at Acıbadem University and presented to hospital leadership.

✚

Background & Motivation

Needlestick injuries are among the most common occupational hazards for healthcare workers, posing serious risks of bloodborne pathogen transmission. Conventional sharps boxes require manual lid operation, leaving staff vulnerable during disposal. This project aimed to fully automate the process.

Design & Development

The prototype was designed in Fusion 360 under the supervision of Dr. Akın, Dean of Engineering at Acıbadem University. The mechanism uses a sensor-triggered lid actuator when a used needle is detected approaching the opening, the lid opens automatically and closes securely once the sharp is deposited, with no hand contact required.

  • Modeled full enclosure and lid mechanism in Fusion 360 with focus on ergonomic placement and clinical footprint
  • Selected materials for compatibility with standard medical sterilization protocols
  • Authored a comprehensive technical report covering functionality, injury risk mitigation rationale, and cost analysis

Outcome

The design was formally presented to Acıbadem Hospitals' product acquisition board. Following the presentation, the prototype was placed under official review for potential adoption across the hospital network a significant outcome for a first engineering internship project.

Orthopedic Implant · 2025

Custom Orthopedic Hip Implant

A custom-designed hip implant modeled from anatomical principles, validated through FEA stress analysis and iterative geometry optimization to meet real physiological performance benchmarks.

⬡

Overview

Total hip arthroplasty is one of the most common and impactful orthopedic procedures. This project focused on designing a hip implant stem optimized for anatomical compatibility, long-term durability, and reduced stress concentrations applying core biomechanical engineering principles throughout.

Design Process

The implant geometry was developed in Fusion 360, starting from anatomical reference data to ensure functional fit within the femoral canal. Careful attention was paid to the proximal shoulder geometry and neck-shaft angle for natural load transfer.

  • Applied anatomical and biomechanical principles to establish implant geometry from reference CT-derived profiles
  • Ran FEA-based stress analysis under simulated physiological loading conditions (gait, stair ascent)
  • Validated safety factors against standard orthopedic structural benchmarks
  • Iteratively refined implant geometry through multiple design cycles to reduce peak stress concentrations
  • Evaluated material candidates for biocompatibility and fatigue resistance (Ti-6Al-4V, CoCrMo)
View 1
View 2

Key Results

The final design achieved structural safety factor targets under all simulated load cases. Stress concentration hotspots identified in early iterations were resolved through geometric fillets and cross-section transitions, resulting in a more uniform stress distribution across the implant body.

Robotics · BU Mars Rover Club · 2025–Present

Mini Robotic Arm

A compact robotic arm designed for BU's Mars Rover life detection assembly, precisely transporting chemical tubes and cuvettes between instruments in a controlled, repeatable linear motion.

⚙

Context

As part of BU's Mars Rover Club life detection team, this robotic arm is a critical subsystem within a larger automated assembly designed to perform chemical experiments on Martian terrain samples. It must operate reliably in a constrained, vibration-prone rover environment with no possibility of in-field correction.

CAD Assembly

Design & Engineering

The arm was modeled in SolidWorks with a strong emphasis on precision motion, repeatability, and low mass. Two primary functions were engineered into the design:

  • Transport of chemical reagent tubes from storage to the mixing carousel for sample introduction
  • Controlled placement of cuvettes into the spectrometer in a precise linear motion path, ensuring optical alignment
  • Minimized degrees of freedom to reduce failure points while achieving required task coverage
  • Designed for integration with the broader life detection assembly frame and actuation system

Engineering Challenges

Key challenges included designing for tight spatial constraints within the rover chassis, ensuring cuvette alignment tolerances within the spectrometer's acceptance window, and selecting actuation methods that remain functional under Mars temperature swings.

Life Detection Engineering · BU Mars Rover Club · 2025–Present

Life Detection Assembly

A full life detection system co-engineered for BU's Mars Rover performing automated chemical experiments on Martian terrain samples through a sealed, multi-stage collection and analysis pipeline.

◎

Mission Context

BU's Mars Rover competes in university rover challenges requiring autonomous life detection from soil samples. The life detection assembly must collect a Martian terrain sample, introduce chemical reagents, perform spectroscopic analysis, and return results all without human intervention and in a rugged, weight-constrained rover platform.

System Design

As co-lead of the life detection team, the assembly was designed as a series of coordinated subsystems:

  • Linear actuation system that drives a sample-collecting auger into the ground and retracts it back into a sealed chamber preventing cross-contamination
  • Sample transfer pathway from the auger into the chemical mixing carousel
  • Integration interface with the mini robotic arm for reagent tube and cuvette handling
  • Full enclosure design to maintain sample integrity across the collection-to-analysis pipeline

Assembly View 2

Engineering Focus

The linear actuation mechanism required careful load analysis to ensure the auger could penetrate compacted soil analogs while remaining reversible and reliable over repeated cycles. Sealing strategy and sample path geometry were critical to ensuring no atmospheric contamination of the collected sample before spectroscopic analysis.

Surgical Robotics · 2025

Soft Robotic Tenaculum

A pneumatically actuated soft robotic tenaculum engineered to replace rigid intrauterine surgical graspers delivering compliant, atraumatic tissue contact to reduce the risk of cervical injury during gynecological procedures.

⌁

Clinical Problem

Conventional tenaculums are rigid, pointed instruments used to grasp and stabilize the cervix during uterine procedures. Their stiff metal construction frequently causes pain, tearing, and bleeding particularly in outpatient settings without anesthesia. A compliant, force-limited alternative could meaningfully improve patient experience and reduce procedural complications.

Design Approach

This project applies soft robotics principles specifically pneumatic elastomeric actuators to create a tenaculum that conforms to tissue geometry and self-limits grasping force through material compliance rather than active control. The goal is a device that is gentler by design, not just by adjustment.

  • Designed soft actuator geometry in CAD to optimize grasping curvature and contact surface area
  • Selected biocompatible elastomeric materials suitable for clinical sterilization and tissue contact
  • Engineered pneumatic channel routing within the actuator body for controlled, symmetric inflation
  • Analyzed grasping force profiles to confirm forces remain below cervical tissue damage thresholds
  • Designed outer form factor for compatibility with existing gynecological instrument access paths

Current Status Prototyping

The project is currently in the physical prototyping stage. Molds have been designed in CAD and are actively being iterated on for casting the elastomeric actuators. The focus right now is on achieving consistent wall thickness, clean pneumatic channel formation, and reliable demolding each iteration informing the next geometry refinement.

Significance

Soft robotic approaches to surgical grasping represent a growing frontier in minimally invasive tools. This project is an ongoing effort to demonstrate how compliant mechanisms can address real patient safety gaps where rigid instrument design falls short.

Sensing · BU Mars Rover Club · 2025

Sensing Vessel

A precision-sealed vessel engineered to house gas and chemical sensors for real-time environmental detection on BU's Mars Rover designed around contamination prevention and seamless integration with the Life Detection Assembly.

◈

Design Context

Detecting life on Mars requires more than collecting a sample the surrounding environment must be characterized to distinguish biological signals from background noise. The Sensing Vessel provides a controlled, sealed enclosure for gas and chemical sensors to monitor ambient and sample-adjacent conditions as part of BU's rover life detection pipeline.

Engineering Approach

The vessel was modeled in Fusion 360 with sealing as the primary constraint. Any atmospheric contamination between the sensor cavity and the Martian environment would corrupt readings, making airtight construction and controlled sensor exposure critical design requirements.

  • Designed sealed enclosure geometry in Fusion 360 to isolate sensor cavity from external contamination
  • Engineered sensor mounting features for repeatable positioning and reliable electrical interface
  • Selected materials and tolerances compatible with Mars temperature cycling and sterilization requirements
  • Designed integration interfaces for mounting directly onto the Life Detection Assembly frame
  • Optimized enclosure form factor for rover weight and spatial constraints

Integration

The Sensing Vessel connects directly to the Life Detection Assembly, providing environmental sensor data alongside the chemical analysis pipeline. The vessel's sealing strategy mirrors the contamination-control principles used across the broader assembly ensuring measurement integrity from sample collection through spectroscopic readout.

Hardware Design · EK 131 · 2025–2026

Temperature Sensing Box Assembly

An ongoing course project building a fully integrated temperature-sensing system from the ground up — enclosure designed in OnShape, components manufactured by hand, and programmed with Arduino to read and output real-time temperature data.

◻

Project Overview

As part of BU's EK 131: Introduction to Engineering, this project is working through the full engineering design-to-build loop. The Temperature Sensing Box Assembly involves designing a custom enclosure in CAD, manufacturing all physical components by hand, wiring electronics, and writing working Arduino code — integrating mechanical, electrical, and software disciplines into one functional system.

Design, Manufacturing & Electronics

The enclosure is being designed in OnShape with geometry optimized for component fit, cable routing, and ease of assembly. Parts are manufactured by hand — cut, shaped, and assembled into the physical box. The electronic circuit connects the temperature sensor to the Arduino, and the firmware reads sensor data and outputs it in real time.

  • Designing the full enclosure in OnShape, modeling component mounting, cable routing, and assembly tolerances
  • Manufacturing all physical parts by hand — cutting, shaping, and assembling the box from raw materials
  • Wiring the temperature sensor and supporting electronics to the Arduino board
  • Writing Arduino firmware to read sensor values, process temperature data, and output results in real time
  • Documenting the full design-to-build process in a technical report covering methodology, fabrication decisions, and measured performance

Current Status

The project is currently in progress as part of the ongoing EK 131 course. The enclosure design is being refined and physical fabrication is underway — with electronics integration and firmware development running in parallel. The goal is a fully functioning, hand-built system that demonstrates end-to-end ownership of the engineering process: designing, building, programming, and documenting a real working device.
